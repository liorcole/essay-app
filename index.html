SECTION I: Soul searching is the core of human motivation

It is my general belief that all human motivation has one source: the desire to shorten the gap between who we are (our identity) and how we live (our reality).

The gap exists because our identity (our mind, soul, spirit) is infinite and abstract—you can't touch, taste, smell, see, or hear it. You can only feel it.

identity
If there was a mask over my face, my mom would still know who I am in a room filled with people through my actions. Who we are is indescribable, it's invisible, it just… is.

Meanwhile, our reality (where we spend our time, our stuff, our physical body and space) is finite: we can directly touch, taste, smell, see, and hear it.

reality
I see the decor I hang on my wall, I can specifically show you what I do for my job, I can smell the salmon I'm cooking in the oven, I recognize my own face in the mirror, etc. These aren't concepts or feelings, our reality is observable fact.

Because our lived reality is finite and limited by nature, we simply can never capture the infinite totality of who we are in our daily lives. Our mind receives signals that help inform us of our identity as we live our lives, though an abstract and encryptedA blind man cannot directly perceive sight- it exists outside his physical abilities. Instead, sight must be translated into what is accessible: like touch or sound. "The apple is round and smooth." "Red feels like the sun's warmth." These descriptions act as bridges, but they never produce a true visual mental model. Core aspects of sight (like color) remain directly unreachable.

This mirrors how our minds receive information about who we are. Identity is not directly observable to us. It is only inferred through signals that surface in our various experiences (emotions, desires, affinities, discomforts) which are translated from the constraints of our biological abilities (sight, smell, touch, taste, sound). There is no sixth sense for soul detection.

Just as the blind man cannot see the vase yet can feel its form, texture, and resonance (and from that, still feel alignment with it), we sense our deeper self through indirect articulations. These moments of resonance are not full comprehension, but they are the closest approximation available within a finite body. modality.

The perpetual discomfort of living in a reality that doesn't fully reflect who we are is the source of human motivation. We are motivated to reduce this gap. The more our reality captures our true identity, the more that discomfort fades. This is the core driver behind everything we do:

A person is struggling with feeling like the way they perceive, think, and relate is fundamentally different from others, yet lack the language to explain how or why. This creates a distinct discomfort: living in a reality that does not reflect how they operate, even at the level of articulating their own experience.
Or think about when you were a kid and felt this drive to redecorate your room. One year princesses and pink, the next punk rock brutalism. Why? Functionally the room is the same - desk, chair, bed. But (whether consciously or unconsciously) we felt that discomfort with our space not matching who we are. A drive to match our reality with our identity. And thus, bits of who we are ends up in the posters lining the walls.
We go to school, we read, we have deep conversations and experiences… Each piece of knowledge and experience teaching us how to articulate the world around us and subsequently who we are within it: bits of ourselves surface when we find language that perfectly describes part of who we are, maybe it's a personality type, sexuality, a moral or political framework.
We chase that ineffable "yes" feeling at concerts, festivals, museums…. Think about the last time you were belting out your favorite song in the car - a bit of your soul is captured in that song and channeled into that chorus.

So we search for pieces of ourselves in the world. When something gives us that ineffable "yes" feeling - a song, a scent, an outfit, a conversation, a new obsession, a person - something clicks. The lingering discomfort of living in a world that only partly embodies who you are is momentarily silenced. A bit of yourself has been channeled into the world around you, and you feel the euphoria of that greater alignment between body and soul.

The gap between our identity and our world can never be fully closedThe gap can never fully close because we can't directly perceive our "soul" and understand it through lived experience the way that we can directly experience a song, a color, a food. Our soul is not something that can be seen, touched, smelled, or heard… But it can be felt.

Take listening to your favorite song "Bohemian Rhapsody" for example:

Listening to "Bohemian Rhapsody" is technically a physical experience: sound waves passing through your ears. But some people feel a surge of euphoria listening, while others hit skip… even though they're hearing the same thing. For that first group, something in the song resonates with their soul. They can't explain why. It just hits. Contrast that with our direct experience of the physical sound - the lyrics, notes, the volume - that's the part we can fully comprehend. We know the exact words being sung, we can identify the exact string of notes. But whether you belt that song out on your morning commute or hit skip and roll your eyes - that's our soul sending us a signal of affinity or repulsion through emotions that are inexplainable. They're inexplainable because, unlike our direct ability to hear (and see, taste, touch, smell), we don't have a sixth sense for soul detection.

This imperfect process - translating something intangible (your soul) into the tangible world - is like trying to describe what a vase looks like to a blind man.

Obviously… he can't see it. Sight exists outside his reality. So he uses what he can perceive directly: the smooth curve under his fingers, the clink of ceramic against the table, the smell of fresh glaze. He builds a picture through touch, sound, smell. It's real. It's useful. But it's built through secondary means - it will never be sight.

Still, he's drawn to certain objects and repelled by others. He develops taste, preference, even passion - all without ever actually seeing the thing in front of him.

This is what we do when we soul-search. We use the tools of our perceivable reality (touch, taste, smell, sight, sound) to make sense of something that exists beyond it. We feel our way toward who we are through songs, words, places, food, clothing - building an indirect picture of our identity that we will never directly comprehend like we could a color.

And that's why the gap never fully closes. We don't have a sixth sense for soul detection. You can't completely embody something that lives outside your reality - the same way the blind man will never form a perfectly accurate aesthetic taste. It's simply not in his world. The soul is simply not in ours.. It's the process of closing that gap - embodying more of our infinite self within the finite world - is the human experience. It's the motivation behind everything*everything meaning after our survival needs are met. Our primary motivation is food, shelter, reproduction (survival). It could be argued that our core desire to survive exists to spend more time channeling more of ourselves into the world, and reproduce to continue this process after death - but that is more of a philosophical thought experiment than what is intended in this essay which is more philosophy for the purpose of practical implementation. Anyway… Our discussion is assuming these needs are met. we do - consciously or not.

So, we are always soul searching. And there is a science to how well we can soul search. It relies entirely on the nature of our access to things, knowledge, and people.

But this isn't reality. Maybe this person never had a car to make it to their dream city. Maybe they weren't aware of the foods that would best nourish their body. The city and the diet exist - they just didn't have access to it.

How much of yourself you can channel into your reality depends entirely on your access to the things, knowledge, and people in the world.

Two forces govern how much access a person has: Availability : the ability to retrieve the things that exist. Accessibility : the ability to reach and leverage resources.

There's a book that would reshape how you see yourself. Availability is whether you have internet access to find and purchase it, and if it will ship to your country. Accessibility is whether it's written in a language you understand.

There's a nutritional balance in food that would make your body feel its best. Availability is whether that food even gets produced in the world, and if there's a way you can actually get it to your door. Accessibility is whether you have the knowledge about your body and nutrition to even recognize the value of this food in your diet, and your ability to actually prepare it in a meal.

Our instinct to soul-search not only drives our individual human motivation, but also our drive to innovate as a species. Together, humanity has always been driven to innovate, which at its core is defined by building instruments that maximize our access to the world's resources.
