SECTION I: Soul searching drives humanity

It is my general belief that all human motivation has one core source: the desire to align who we are (our identity) with how we live (our reality).

The gap exists because our identity (our mind, soul, spirit) is infinite and abstract - you can't touch, taste, smell, see, or hear it. You can only feel it.

identity
If there was a mask over my face, my mom would still know who I am in a room filled with people through my actions. Who we are is indescribable, it's invisible, it just… is.

Meanwhile, our reality (where we spend our time, our stuff, our physical body and space) is finite: we can directly touch, taste, smell, see, and hear it.

reality
I see the decor I hang on my wall, I can specifically show you what I do for my job, I can smell the salmon I'm cooking in the oven, I recognize my own face in the mirror, etc. These aren't concepts or feelings, our reality is observable fact.

Because our reality is finite and limited by nature, our lives around us simply can never capture the infinite totality of who we are...

A person is struggling with feeling like the way they perceive, think, and relate is fundamentally different from others, yet lack the language to explain how or why. This creates a distinct discomfort: living in a reality that does not reflect how they operate, even at the level of articulating their own experience.
Or think about when you were a kid and felt this drive to redecorate your room. One year princesses and pink, the next punk rock brutalism. Why? Functionally the room is the same - desk, chair, bed. But (whether consciously or unconsciously) we felt that discomfort with our space not matching who we are. A drive to match our reality with our identity. And thus, bits of who we are ends up in the posters lining the walls.
We go to school, we read, we have deep conversations and experiences… Each piece of knowledge and experience teaching us how to articulate the world around us and subsequently who we are within it: bits of ourselves surface when we find language that perfectly describes part of who we are, maybe it's a personality type, sexuality, a moral or political framework.
We chase that ineffable "yes" feeling at concerts, festivals, museums…. Think about the last time you were belting out your favorite song in the car - a bit of your soul is captured in that song and channeled into that chorus.

Your lived experience made up of your stuff, career, favorite song, passions, friends, actions, knowledge, opinions is your reality. It reflects parts of you, but could never capture the full depth of who you are… Look at all the uncaptured identity!

The posters on my wall say something about me, or when I say I like “mid century modern” that outlines a bit more of my mind, but no amount of decor or words could ever capture the full depth of who I am. Reflect parts - yes. Full picture - impossible. Your soul is greater than all the words or stuff or knowledge or people or anything else that exists on this Earth. Your soul is infinite, this world is finite.

In contrast, if I look at a red apple - I know it’s red. It’s observable fact that exists in reality. Our identity is so complex for us to grasp because you can’t touch, taste, smell, see, or hear your soul - it exists somewhere beyond our lived experience. We don’t have a sixth sense for soul detection. Because of this, we understand and channel who we are indirectly:

A blind man cannot directly perceive sight – the visual world exists outside his reality. So to get a sense of what something “looks” like, he relies on what he can experience, like touch or sound: he can feel a vase that is round and smooth, the sound it makes when he taps it with his finger, he can interpret anecdotes like “its red color feels like the sun’s warmth”. He can choose which vase he likes best of all the options at a pottery shop without ever seeing any of them, based on his indirect experiences.

This indirect modality is how we shape our sense of self since our soul exists beyond our lived reality. Our mind does this for us by sending us signals through emotion and intuition as we navigate the world: obsessed with that song, gut feeling about that person, interested in that subject, repulsed by that shirt... We may not ever grasp our identity the way we can directly and fully grasp the color red, but over time our mind helps us paint a picture of who we are through what we are drawn to and repelled by within our direct world... Just as the blind man develops aesthetic taste indirectly through touch, smell, sound, and taste.

The process of finding ourselves in the world through experiences, knowledge, and stuff is always happening. We feel a pressing, underlying discomfort when we live in a way that doesn’t match who we are: that feeling of knowing you’re in the wrong career, wearing a shirt you hate, putting on a persona for certain family members or friends that simply isn’t you. No one can sit in the discomfort of that dissonance forever, so it drives us to do something about it. Sometimes it’s as straight forward as changing the shirt you hate to one you like. Often it’s not as clear - if we feel a general sense of wanting more or feeling out of place without a clear reason, we will do irrational things like get a crazy hair cut, quit your job, move to a totally new city, become completely immersed in a random new hobby, buy a sports car. Each thing is another spin of the roulette wheel that may land you on something that channels a bit more of who you are in the world, that would give you that temporary burst of alignment energy.

Because our lived reality is too limited to ever fully embody all of who we are, our internal misalignment discomfort never goes away… Because all of who we are will never fully align with our world. Consciously or subconsciously, we are always chasing relief from that discomfort, which comes from momentary alignment when we define and channel a bit more of ourselves into the world around us. All human motivation has one core source: the desire to align who we are with how we live. It drives everything that we do:

We redecorate our rooms a million times over, until the posters on our walls feel like us. We get a better sense of who we are when we can feel ourselves in the space we design around us.
We search for labels that help articulate who we are: a diagnosis, personality type, sexuality, a belief system. Every label that fits pulls a bit of our nebulous, out-of-reach identity into concrete reality through language.
We chase the ineffable “yes” feeling at concerts, festivals, museums— that moment belting our favorite song in the car where something unnamed inside us is briefly captured.
We buy new clothes to reflect our ever evolving inner state.
We seek out knowledge to help our mind better decipher who we are: we go to school, read books and articles, we have deep conversation and experiences. Knowledge gives us insight to better understand the world and subsequently ourselves within it— what do we like? What do we agree with? What do we desire?

But why?

There’s no tactical use for decor, no real reason to have 50 different shirt styles, no actual need for music or even knowledge beyond information related to food and shelter. Every other species seems to understand this: lions are out hunting prey, beavers are building dams, butterflies finding nectar… None are spending time or resources decorating their homes with funky colors or writing a thesis on shakespearean theatrical semiotics. So why did we evolve past the universal drive to survive into this complex and seemingly unnecessary universe of soul searching and identity formation?

My personal theory begins at the genesis of our species. Homo sapiens were not the only humans - there were Neanderthals, Denisovans, Homo erectus, Homo floresiensis, among others. Research suggests that due to differences in brain structure, Homo sapiens were the only species capable of conceptualizing the intangible (early belief systems, ideologies, mathematical and scientific concepts). Every other human species was confined to what was right in front of them: eat the berry on the tree, run from the predator behind me.

This was a huge distinction. Humans can maintain about 150 stable relationships. Not acquaintances - relationships where you track trust, history, and reciprocity. Without anything larger than personal bonds to organize groups of people, every tribe was therefore limited in size: you can only be in the tribe if there’s a personal relationship, and the number of those we can handle at once is limited. However, Homo sapiens didn’t need to rely on personal connection to establish trust. They could congregate around shared identity because their brains could handle the intangible concept of identity - religion, mythology, belief - the size of their tribes became limitless. Today this looks like - if you are a Jew and I am a Jew, we don’t need to know each other to belong to the same group, essentially infinite in size. The sheer imbalance in tribal size was enough for Homo sapiens to wipe everyone else out.

So, in theory, a powerful connection to your identity was arguably the number one trait for human survival. Without a strong sense and alliance to your identity, you wouldn’t align with a massive tribe organized on shared values and instead seek safety in direct relationships… Placing you in one of the smaller tribes bound to defeat and extinction. If all this logic is sound, that would mean we are quite literally wired to prioritize intangible concepts we align with (which make up our identity) above all else. There is so much evidence around us today that would support this: why is the number one cause of war religion? Why is the power of nationalism so strong that people risk their own lives for their country? Why does the apple logo hold such a strong grip over billions of people even if Android makes a far superior product? Logically borders and belief systems should have little impact on society - people will live in that plot of land whether it’s labeled Thailand or Cambodia, the religious beliefs your neighbor has shouldn’t technically impact your life in any way. The fact that my sock has a nike swoosh on it doesn’t actually change the sock… But for us, it does.

We are wired to develop and embody the layers of our identity so intensely it feels almost like our life depends on it... because at one point it did.

Regardless of why you believe our innate drive to define and embody our identity in the world exists, the fact is that it does. We all feel it. There’s a pull in all of us towards meeting new people, learning new things, gaining new experiences - for a reason that exceeds direct logic. If there wasn’t, we’d all be wearing the same optimized weather and germ protective clothes, live in safety and productivity optimized homes, share and consume only survival related information and search for a partner on the exclusive basis of producing the strongest offspring. That world is efficient. It’s also not human.

Instead, we live driven by forces larger than survival - constantly working to find and express ourselves in the world around us.

In Section II, we will explore how human innovation has evolved since the beginning to help us satisfy our innate burning desire to soul search. This will contextualize the technological landscape we exist within today, which will help us identify where we are headed next in the tumultuous era of AI and beyond.

We’ve established that everything we do, at our core, is motivated by our innate desire to define and channel who we are into the world around us. To be human is to be in a perpetual state of soul searching.

This drive powers everything - not only our individual behaviors, but also the way humanity functions at large. Humankind has progressed not because of our physical strength but because of the innovation we consistently create around ourselves, and, at its core, all*all meaning after our survival needs are met. human innovation is fundamentally an instrument to help us soul search: the printing press was born out of our chase for knowledge. The cascade of new product variations, clothing styles, and decor that poured out of the factories developed in the Industrial Revolution helps satisfy our need to see ourselves reflected in the design of the world around us. Communication tools - from the postal system to the telephone to the internet - to spread the ideas and knowledge we use to understand ourselves and each other.

So while we are personally be driven to seek new experiences, people, things, and knowledge, the technology we’ve built allows us do that in a perpetually increasing capacity. Innovation isn’t just about solving individual problems; it’s about building an instrument to externalize our identity.

Before the internet, the limiting factor to channeling yourself in the world was access: to information, people, and things.

…If you lived in a small village in 4000 BCE, your access to stuff would be limited by what the local craftsman created. So even if your inner spirit was screaming for a punk rock and gothic aesthetic, that part of yourself would never be realized or expressed because your clothing choices were either a wool cloak or a linen tunic. You’d live out your life with this lingering feeling that something was missing without access to the knowledge or resources that aligns with a part of who you are.

Your home would be whatever the carpenter was able to build, your knowledge confined to stories passed down, food was what the farmer and land produced, job options limited to a handful of roles - craftsman, trade, agriculture, etc. Every person has an innate identity inside of them, and we all share a desire to live in alignment with that spirit. But we can only embody who we are to the extent of our access to the knowledge, people, or stuff that aligns with us.

Out of everything that exists in the world, there is a most aligned path for you: a favorite song, a most fulfilling career, a strongest passion, an optimal diet, a game that makes you happiest, a city you thrive most in, a life partner you are most compatible with, the most flattering and aligned outfit… Most of these things you haven't found yet. Many you never will. You may have a song you love, passions, a defined diet, etc. But it's rare that these things are actually the most aligned. It's rare that the song you love is actually the one that would resonate strongest out of all songs that exist in the world, or that your passions are truly the most invigorating of every possible activity on earth… Why?

But this isn't reality. Maybe this person never had a car to make it to their dream city. Maybe they weren't aware of the foods that would best nourish their body. The city and the diet exist - they just didn't have access to it.

Two forces govern how much "access" a person has to the stuff in the world:
1. Availability: the ability to retrieve the things that exist.
2. Accessibility: the ability to leverage the resources available to you.

For example, let's say there's a specific book that would best help you figure something out about who you are, which will then lead you to having more fulfilling relationships with other people and a more fulfilling career (AKA a life lived in greater alignment between identity and lived experience). Availability is whether you have internet access to find and purchase the book and if it will ship to your country. Accessibility is whether it's written in a language you understand, and in a style you can fully grasp.
Another example- imagine you are a tech whiz and computers are your innate life passion, you just don't know it yet. Availability is if there is a computer store you're able to travel to + a computer you can afford to buy. Accessibility is to what extent you can figure out how to actually use that computer. (If this was the 1980s… there's a good chance that even if you could get your hands on a computer, you wouldn't ever realize your tech whiz abilities and passion given THIS is what you'd be working with. Poor accessibility would block your ability to channel a core part of your identity into the world around you → you live in less alignment).

Our instinct to soul-search not only drives our individual human motivation, but also our drive to innovate as a species. From the printing press to AI and everything in between, all the technology we have ever produced is an instrument for soul-searching at its core: a way to expand how much of the world we can access, and therefore how much of ourselves we can find within it.
